About AirAware

A collaborative GIS project on air pollution in Hungary by three students from the MSc Geoinformatics Engineering program at Politecnico di Milano.

The project aims to:

  • Investigate long-term air quality trends through data analysis.
  • Evaluate the potential impact of pollution on the population.
  • Share insights through an interactive platform with visual tools.

AirAware collects air quality data from open sources and sensor networks and presents it in a user-friendly format. Through interactive maps and visual analytics, the platform offers a clear and accessible view of air quality, supporting a deeper understanding of historical trends and exposure patterns. With transparent access to air quality data, AirAware encourages informed communities to advocate for healthier environments.

Unforgiving Footprint

Understanding the long-term impact of air pollution on health, ecosystems, and future generations.

Source: World Health Organization. Usage Policy.

Air pollution remains a significant environmental and public health issue in Hungary, with particulate matter (PM2.5 and PM10) and nitrogen dioxide (NO2) among the most critical pollutants. Major urban centers such as Budapest, Miskolc, and Debrecen frequently report concentrations that approach or exceed World Health Organization (WHO) guideline levels.

These pollutants are linked to increased rates of respiratory and cardiovascular diseases, reduced life expectancy, and environmental degradation. Long-term exposure affects not only human health, but also urban ecosystems, agriculture, and climate.
Monitoring their presence and evolution is essential to understanding local impacts and guiding effective, evidence-based responses.

The legacy of air pollution is written not just in data, but in lives, landscapes, and future generations. Knowing where, when, and how these pollutants accumulate is key to understanding long-term exposure and making informed, forward-looking decisions.

Pollutants Explained

Get to know the pollutants monitored by AirAware and their impact on health and the environment.

Particulate matter 2.5

PM2.5 refers to fine inhalable particles with a diameter of 2.5 micrometers or smaller. These are primarily made up of substances like black carbon, sulfates, nitrates, and organic chemicals.

  • Emitted by vehicles, industrial processes, residential heating, and wildfires.
  • Penetrates deep into the lungs and bloodstream, contributing to heart and respiratory diseases.
  • Often elevated during combustion peaks and atmospheric stagnation, reducing visibility and harming sensitive ecosystems through deposition.

Particulate matter 10

PM10 refers to inhalable particles with a diameter of 10 micrometers or smaller These are primarly made up of particles like dust, pollen, and mold.

  • Emitted by construction sites, unpaved roads, and industrial activities.
  • Can worsen lung function and trigger allergic reactions.
  • Often elevated during dry, windy conditions, contributing to haze and soil/water contamination.

Nitrogen Dioxide

NO2 is a reactive gas formed primarily from the combustion of fossil fuels at high temperatures. It is a major component of urban air pollution.

  • Emitted by vehicles, power plants, and industrial sources.
  • Irritates the respiratory system and worsens asthma symptoms.
  • Often elevated during traffic congestion and weather stagnation, contributing to smog formation, acid rain, and ecosystem damage.

Why It Matters

Understanding air pollution is essential not just for researchers, but for communities, decision-makers, and individuals.

  • Citizens and communities: understand air quality trends in your area and advocate for cleaner environments.
  • Health professionals: integrate exposure data into public health assessments and prevention strategies.
  • Urban planners: design greener spaces and plan developments in areas with lower pollution exposure.
  • Policy-makers: use spatial evidence to support air quality regulations and long-term climate goals.
  • Researchers: validate models, compare pollution datasets, and analyze long-term trends in land-use impact.

Source: CAMS Reanalysis. Usage Policy.
